# Herd Dynamics Reference

## Understanding Herd Behavior

Horses are herd animals to their core. Their social structure and relationships affect every aspect of their behavior — under saddle, in the barn, and in the field.

## Hierarchy

### How It Works
- Linear hierarchy: Horse A dominates B, B dominates C, etc.
- Established through body language, not usually fighting
- Resource control: dominant horse eats first, drinks first, chooses best spot
- Leadership is NOT always the most dominant horse — the leader is often a calm, older mare

### How to Map Hierarchy
- Who moves whom away from food/water/shade?
- Who goes through gates first?
- Who gets the preferred resting spot?
- Who grooms whom first? (lower-ranked horse often initiates grooming)
- Who pins ears at whom?

## Pair Bonds

Horses form deep bonds with specific companions:
- Bonded pairs stand close, groom each other, travel together
- Separation causes intense distress (calling, running fence, not eating, anxiety)
- Losing a bonded partner to death or relocation = genuine grief
- Track: who is bonded with whom, how they show it, separation tolerance

## Common Herd Behaviors

### Mutual Grooming
- Pairs face opposite directions and scratch each other's withers/neck
- Indicates trust and social bonding
- Track who grooms whom — reveals social preferences

### Play
- Running together, rearing, play-biting
- More common in young horses and geldings
- Some horses are playful, others are serious
- Track: who initiates play, who participates, play style

### Aggression
- Ear pinning (warning)
- Snaking (lowered head, herding others)
- Kicking (hind leg strike — can cause serious injury)
- Biting (can be playful or aggressive — context matters)
- Striking (front leg — less common but dangerous)
- Track: who is aggressive, triggers, targets, severity

### Buddy/Barn Sour
- Horse refuses to leave herd companions or barn area
- Under saddle: spinning, balking, calling, rushing home
- Severity ranges from mild reluctance to dangerous behavior
- Management: gradual desensitization, building rider trust, exercise away from barn

## Turnout Management

### What to Track
- Turnout companions and any changes
- Time in vs time out
- Injuries from turnout (kicks, bites, scrapes)
- Behavior in turnout (grazes, plays, stands at gate, runs fence)
- How turnout changes affect behavior under saddle

### Introduction to New Herd
- NEVER just throw a new horse in with an established group
- Protocol: visual introduction over fence → supervised time with one calm horse → gradual group integration
- Track: how existing horses react, any aggression, how long until settled

## Special Considerations

### Mares
- Hormonal cycles affect behavior (moody, sensitive, distracted when in heat)
- Some mares are aggressive toward other horses when in season
- Track: cycle timing and behavior changes

### Stallions
- Not typically kept with other horses (aggressive to geldings, dangerous with mares)
- Highly social needs despite isolation — many develop behavioral issues from loneliness
- Handling requires experience and safety protocols

### Senior Horses
- May drop in hierarchy as they age
- Need protection from being bullied away from food
- May need separate turnout with a calm companion
- Dental and mobility issues affect their ability to compete for resources
