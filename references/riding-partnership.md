# Riding Partnership Reference

## What Makes the Horse-Human Partnership Unique

Unlike any other pet, horses are a WORKING partnership. You don't just live with a horse — you communicate through physical contact while moving at speed. The riding relationship has layers:

### Physical Communication
- Seat (weight distribution in the saddle)
- Legs (pressure, position, timing)
- Hands (rein contact, giving and taking)
- Voice (clucks, whoa, verbal cues)
- Every horse responds to these aids differently — mapping what YOUR horse responds to is key

### Trust Building
- Horses are prey animals — trust must be earned through consistency
- Groundwork builds trust before riding
- Spooky situations test and build trust
- The horse needs to trust the rider's judgment, and vice versa

## Tracking Riding Sessions

### What to Log Per Session
- Date and time
- Duration
- Discipline/type (flatwork, jumping, trail, liberty, lunging)
- What went well (specific — "right lead canter was balanced for 20m circle")
- What was difficult (specific — "kept breaking to trot in left lead transition")
- Horse's energy/mood (fresh, lazy, focused, distracted, tense)
- Weather/conditions (hot, cold, windy, muddy, indoor vs outdoor)
- Any notable events (spooked at X, first time doing Y, great moment doing Z)

### Progress Tracking Dimensions
For each skill area, track:
- Current level (beginner → advanced scale)
- Recent trajectory (improving, plateaued, regressing)
- Specific milestones achieved
- Current challenges
- What helps (specific aids, exercises, warm-up routines)

### Common Skill Areas by Discipline

**Dressage:** Walk/trot/canter quality, transitions, lateral work (leg yield, shoulder-in, half-pass), collection, extension, specific test movements

**Jumping:** Ground poles, crossrails, verticals, oxers, combinations, course work, distances/striding, bravery, scope

**Western:** Stop, spin, rollback, lead change, loping circles, trail patterns, roping readiness

**Trail:** Water crossings, bridges, steep terrain, traffic, wildlife encounters, alone vs group riding

## Bad Habits and Solutions

### Common Under-Saddle Issues
- Rushing (especially to fences or back to the barn)
- Dropping shoulder (falling in on turns)
- Behind the leg (not responding to aids promptly)
- Above the bit (head up, evading contact)
- Bucking (pain, freshness, or behavioral)
- Rearing (DANGEROUS — seek professional help)
- Bolting (DANGEROUS — may need equipment or training intervention)
- Barn/buddy sour (won't leave barn or companions)

### Tracking Improvement
- Log each incident with context (what triggered it, how you handled it, outcome)
- Note what strategies work and what doesn't
- Track frequency over time — are incidents decreasing?
- Professional trainer involvement and their recommendations
