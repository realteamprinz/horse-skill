# Horse Health Tracking Reference

## Why Horse Health Tracking Matters

Horses are masters at hiding pain and illness (prey animal instinct). By the time they show obvious signs, the problem is often advanced. Tracking baselines and subtle changes catches issues early.

## Body Condition Scoring (Henneke Scale)

Score from 1 (emaciated) to 9 (extremely fat):
- 1-3: Underweight — ribs, spine, hip bones visible
- 4-5: Ideal — ribs easily felt but not visible, smooth appearance
- 6: Moderately fleshy — fat beginning to deposit
- 7-9: Overweight to obese — cresty neck, fat pads, health risk

Track monthly with photos and scoring.

## Lameness Detection

### The Head Bob Test
- If the horse's HEAD drops when a FRONT leg hits the ground → that front leg is sound (they drop their head to unload the painful leg)
- If the horse's HIP drops more on one side → the opposite HIND leg may be painful
- Lameness grading: 0 (sound) to 5 (non-weight-bearing)

### Common Lameness Causes
- Hoof abscess (sudden, severe, usually resolves)
- Navicular syndrome (gradual, bilateral front, serious)
- Tendon/ligament injury (sudden, often with swelling)
- Arthritis/joint issues (gradual, worsens with cold)
- Stone bruise (sudden, improves with rest)
- Laminitis (EMERGENCY — hot hooves, rocked-back stance, reluctance to move)

### What to Track
- Which leg(s)
- When it appears (cold, after work, on hard ground, in circles, always)
- Severity (grade 0-5)
- Duration (intermittent, constant, getting better/worse)
- What helps (rest, bute, different footing, wraps)
- Vet findings and treatments

## Hoof Care

### Farrier Schedule
- Every 6-8 weeks (shoeing or trimming)
- Track: date, what was done, any issues, farrier notes
- Hoof quality changes over time
- Shoe type and modifications if applicable

### Hoof Issues
- Thrush (foul smell in frog area, especially in wet conditions)
- Abscess (sudden severe lameness, heat in hoof, usually resolves when drained)
- Cracks (horizontal, vertical, superficial, or structural)
- White line disease
- Laminitis/founder (rotation of coffin bone — EMERGENCY)

## Vaccination Schedule

Core vaccines (annual minimum):
- Eastern/Western Encephalomyelitis
- Tetanus
- West Nile Virus
- Rabies

Risk-based vaccines (depending on region and use):
- Influenza
- Rhinopneumonitis (EHV-1/4)
- Strangles
- Potomac Horse Fever
- Botulism

## Deworming

Modern approach: Fecal egg count (FEC) testing to determine IF and WHAT to deworm with, rather than rotational deworming on a schedule.
- FEC testing 2-4 times per year
- Only deworm horses with significant egg counts
- Track which dewormer used and when (to monitor resistance)

## Dental Care

- Dental exam every 6-12 months
- Horses' teeth continuously erupt and develop sharp points
- Signs of dental issues: dropping food (quidding), head tossing, resistance to bit, weight loss
- Track: exam dates, findings, procedures (floating), age-related changes

## Colic

The #1 killer of horses. Track risk factors:
- Previous colic episodes (increases future risk)
- Changes in routine (feed, turnout, travel)
- Stress events
- Sand ingestion (sandy soil areas)
- Dehydration risk
- Gas colic vs impaction vs displacement vs torsion (severity varies enormously)
- Signs: pawing, rolling, looking at flanks, not eating, no gut sounds
- ALWAYS treat as emergency until vet clears
